Brand Strategy
Why a brand refresh could be the best investment your business makes in 2025

Published June 2025  |  8 minute read

There is a conversation that happens in many businesses at the start of each year. The marketing budget gets reviewed, the priorities are debated, and somewhere in the list there is usually a line item that reads something like "look at the brand" or "update the website." It gets pushed back again and again -- there are always more urgent things to spend the money on. If this sounds familiar, you are not alone. And if this has been happening for more than two or three years, it is worth pausing to consider what it is actually costing you.

What does an ageing brand actually cost you?

The cost of an outdated brand is almost never visible as a single line item. Nobody receives an invoice for "credibility lost due to poor logo design." Instead, the cost manifests in subtler ways: a slightly lower conversion rate on your website. A pitch that almost came off but did not. A talented recruit who chose a competitor that looked more established. A client who chose not to renew because the relationship felt less dynamic than it once did.

These are difficult to measure directly, which is why the case for brand investment tends to get deprioritised. But the accumulation of small disadvantages over months and years can represent a significant commercial drag on a business that, in every other respect, is performing well.

Signs that your brand needs attention

There are several indicators that a brand has reached the point where investment is warranted. The most obvious is age: a brand created more than ten years ago was designed for a different commercial environment, different screen sizes and different audience expectations. But age alone is not the problem -- some brands age beautifully because they were built on genuinely strong foundations. The real issue is whether your brand is still accurately representing your business as it is today.

If your business has grown significantly since your brand was last updated, there is likely a mismatch. If your competitors have invested in their brands and yours has not kept pace, the gap will be noticeable to your shared audience even if it is not noticeable to you. If you feel slightly apologetic when you hand over a business card or show a prospect your website, that instinct is worth listening to.

What a brand refresh actually involves

A brand refresh is not a rebrand. It does not mean starting from scratch or abandoning the equity your existing brand has built. In most cases, it means evolving what you have: refining the logo, updating the colour system, modernising the typography, sharpening the brand guidelines. The result should feel like the same business -- just the best version of it.

The process typically starts with a strategic review: what does the brand currently communicate, and what do you want it to communicate? Are there gaps between perception and reality? Are there competitors who have positioned themselves in ways that require you to reconsider your own positioning? This strategic groundwork is what separates a cosmetic update from a genuinely valuable refresh.

The return on investment

Brand investment returns are real but indirect, which makes them uncomfortable to quantify in advance. What we can say with confidence, based on twenty years of working with businesses across Hampshire and the South, is that businesses that invest in their brands consistently outperform those that do not -- not because great design is magic, but because it signals the competence and attention to detail that clients use to assess suppliers before they have experienced the service itself.

The cost of a well-executed brand refresh, spread over the years it serves you, is rarely as significant as it appears at the point of investment. The question is not whether you can afford to do it. The question is whether you can afford not to.
